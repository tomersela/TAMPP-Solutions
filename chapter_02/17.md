### Lemma. If at most $m$ threads visit a Bouncer object, then at most $m-1$ threads leave it to the RIGHT, and at most $m-1$ threads leave it to the DOWN.

**Proof:**  
This is exactly Exercise 16: at most one thread gets `STOP`, at most $m-1$ get `RIGHT`, and at most $m-1$ get `DOWN`.

### Lemma. Any Bouncer object at level $k$ is visited by at most $n-k$ threads.

Here level $0$ contains only Bouncer $0$, level $1$ contains the two Bouncers below it, and in general level $k$ contains $k+1$ Bouncers.

**Proof:**  
We prove this by induction on $k$.

**Base case ($k=0$):**  
Only Bouncer $0$ is at level $0$, and all $n$ threads start there, so it is visited by at most $n = n-0$ threads.

**Induction step:**  
Assume every Bouncer at level $k$ is visited by at most $n-k$ threads.  
Take any Bouncer $b$ at level $k+1$. It is reached from one Bouncer in the previous level, either from above-left by a `DOWN` outcome or from above-right by a `RIGHT` outcome. By the induction hypothesis, that predecessor is visited by at most $n-k$ threads. By the previous lemma, at most $n-k-1$ threads can leave that predecessor in the relevant direction. Hence $b$ is visited by at most
$$
n-k-1 = n-(k+1).
$$

So the claim holds for level $k+1$.

### Theorem. Every thread eventually stops at some Bouncer object.

**Proof:**  
Consider level $n-1$, the bottom level of the triangular array. By the previous lemma, every Bouncer at level $n-1$ is visited by at most
$$
n-(n-1)=1
$$
thread.

Now consider a Bouncer visited by exactly one thread. That thread writes `last = i`, then reads `goRight`. Since no other thread visits that Bouncer, `goRight` is still `false`, so the thread does not return `RIGHT`. It sets `goRight = true`, and since no other thread has written `last`, it reads `last == i` and returns `STOP`.

Therefore any thread that reaches the bottom level must stop there. Since every move goes from level $k$ to level $k+1$, a thread cannot move forever; by the time it reaches level $n-1$, it must stop. Hence every thread eventually stops at some Bouncer object.

### Corollary. If the total number of threads is known in advance to be $n$, then the array needs
$$
1+2+\cdots+n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}
$$
Bouncer objects.

**Proof:**  
The triangular array has $n$ levels, numbered $0,1,\dots,n-1$. Level $k$ contains $k+1$ Bouncer objects. Therefore the total number of Bouncers is
$$
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(k+1)=1+2+\cdots+n=\frac{n(n+1)}{2}.
$$

By the theorem, this many levels are sufficient to guarantee that all $n$ threads stop.
