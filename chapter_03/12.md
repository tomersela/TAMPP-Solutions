Sequential consistency is a nonblocking property: a pending invocation of a total method is never required to wait for another pending invocation to complete.

**Theorem.** If $m$ is a total method of an object $x$ and $⟨x.m(a^\ast) \; P⟩$ is a pending invocation in a sequentially consistent history $H$, then there exists a response $⟨x:t(r^\ast) \; P⟩$ such that $H \cdot ⟨x:t(r^\ast) \; P⟩$ is sequentially consistent.

**Proof.** Let $S$ be any sequentially consistent ordering of $H$. If $S$ includes a response to $⟨x.m(a^\ast) \; P⟩$, we are done, since $S$ is also a valid ordering of $H \cdot ⟨x:t(r^\ast) \; P⟩$. Otherwise, $⟨x.m(a^\ast) \; P⟩$ does not appear in $S$ either, since a sequential ordering has no pending invocations. Because $m$ is total, there exists a response $⟨x:t(r^\ast) \; P⟩$ such that:

$$S' = S \cdot ⟨x.m(a^\ast) \; P⟩ \cdot ⟨x:t(r^\ast) \; P⟩$$

is a legal sequential history. $S'$ preserves per-thread program order: the invocation was the last event from thread $P$ in $H$, so appending it at the end of $S$ does not reorder any of $P$'s earlier operations. Therefore $S'$ is a sequentially consistent ordering of $H \cdot ⟨x:t(r^\ast) \; P⟩$.

Note: the linearizability proof requires additionally that real-time order is preserved. Here we only need per-thread program order, which makes the argument simpler — we do not need to worry about where the new invocation falls relative to other threads' operations.
