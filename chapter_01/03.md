Since Bob can't see the state of Alice’s can, one can is no longer enough. Bob needs an explicit acknowledgment that the previous batch of food has been consumed. The solution is to use **two cans**:

- a **food-available can** on Alice’s windowsill, which **Bob** can knock down,
- an **empty-yard can** on Bob’s windowsill, which **Alice** can knock down.

Initially, the empty-yard can is **down** (meaning Bob is allowed to place food because no food is currently waiting), and the food-available can is **up**.

### Protocol

**Bob (producer):**
1. Bob waits until the empty-yard can on his side is down.
2. He enters the yard and places food there.
3. He leaves the yard.
4. He resets the empty-yard can to **up**.
5. He pulls the string to knock down the food-available can on Alice’s side.

**Alice (consumer side):**
1. Alice waits until the food-available can is down.
2. She releases the pets into the yard.
3. When the pets return, she checks that the food is gone.
4. She resets the food-available can to **up**.
5. She knocks down Bob’s empty-yard can to signal that the previous food has been consumed and Bob may place more food.

### Why it works

This protocol is a **two-way handshake**.

- Bob does not place new food until Alice has signaled that the old food is gone.
- Alice does not release the pets until Bob has signaled that food has been placed.
- Thus, the pets never enter the yard unless food is there.
- Bob never places more food while old food remains.
- Since Bob leaves the yard before signaling food available, and Alice releases the pets only after that signal, Bob and the pets are never in the yard together.
