Amdahl’s Law states:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{k}}
$$

where:

- $p$ is the fraction of the original runtime spent in the part being improved
- $k$ is the speedup factor for that part

When the improvement comes specifically from parallelization over $n$ processors, this becomes:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{n}}
$$

Assume, where needed, that the sequential program’s total execution time is $1$ (unit time).


### Question

> Suppose $M$ accounts for $30\%$ of the program’s execution time and runs sequentially. What is the limit for the overall speedup that can be achieved on an $n$-processor machine?

### Solution

If $30\%$ cannot be parallelized, the parallelizable fraction is:

$$
p = 0.7
$$

So:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{n}}
  = \frac{1}{0.3 + \frac{0.7}{n}}
$$

As $n \to \infty$, the term $\frac{0.7}{n}$ tends to $0$, so the maximum possible speedup is:

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} S = \frac{1}{0.3} = \frac{10}{3}
$$

### Final answer

$$
S_{\max} = \frac{10}{3}
$$

So the program can never be sped up by more than a factor of $\frac{10}{3}$ by adding processors, no matter how large $n$ is.


### Question

> Suppose $M$ accounts for $40\%$ of the program’s execution time. You replace $M$ with $M'$, which is $k$ times faster than $M$. What value of $k$ yields an overall speedup of $2$ for the whole program?

### Solution

Let the original runtime be $1$. Method $M$ uses $40\%$ of that time, and the rest uses $60\%$. After the replacement, the time spent in what was $M$ is $\frac{0.4}{k}$, and the rest is unchanged. The overall speedup is therefore:

$$
S = \frac{1}{0.6 + \frac{0.4}{k}}
$$

Set $S = 2$:

$$
2 = \frac{1}{0.6 + \frac{0.4}{k}}
$$

$$
0.6 + \frac{0.4}{k} = 0.5
$$

$$
\frac{0.4}{k} = -0.1
$$

No positive $k$ satisfies this.

Even if $M'$ were infinitely fast ($k \to \infty$), the sequential part still needs at least $60\%$ of the original time, so the best possible speedup is:

$$
\frac{1}{0.6} = \frac{5}{3} \approx 1.67
$$

which is less than $2$.

### Final answer

$$
\text{Impossible}
$$

There is no finite (or infinite) $k$ that doubles overall speed while $60\%$ of the original runtime remains untouched.


### Question

> Suppose $M'$, the replacement for $M$, runs $4$ times as fast as $M$. What fraction of the overall execution time (of the original sequential program) must $M$ account for so that replacing $M$ with $M'$ doubles the program’s speed?

### Solution

Let $f$ be the fraction of original unit time spent in $M$, so the rest is $1 - f$. After replacement:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1 - f) + \frac{f}{4}}
$$

Set $S = 2$:

$$
2 = \frac{1}{(1 - f) + \frac{f}{4}}
$$

$$
(1 - f) + \frac{f}{4} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
1 - \frac{3f}{4} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
\frac{3f}{4} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
f = \frac{2}{3}
$$

### Final answer

$$
f = \frac{2}{3}
$$

So $M$ must originally account for two thirds of the execution time for a four-fold speedup on that part to double the speed of the whole program.
