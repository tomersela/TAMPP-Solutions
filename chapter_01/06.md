Amdahl’s Law states:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{k}}
$$

where:

- $p$ is the fraction of the original runtime spent in the part being improved
- $k$ is the speedup factor for that part

When the improvement comes specifically from parallelization over $n$ processors, this becomes:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{n}}
$$


### Question

> Suppose a program has a method $M$ that cannot be parallelized, and that this method accounts for $40\%$ of the execution time. What is the limit for the overall speedup on an $n$-processor machine?

### Solution

If $40\%$ cannot be parallelized, then the parallelizable fraction is:

$$
p = 0.6
$$

So:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{n}}
  = \frac{1}{0.4 + \frac{0.6}{n}}
$$

As $n \to \infty$, the term $\frac{0.6}{n}$ tends to $0$, so the maximum possible speedup is:

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} S = \frac{1}{0.4} = 2.5
$$

### Final answer

$$
S_{\max} = 2.5
$$

So the program can never be sped up by more than a factor of $2.5$, regardless of how many processors are added.


### Question

> Suppose method $M$ accounts for $30\%$ of the program’s computation time. What speedup of $M$ is required in order to improve the overall execution time by a factor of $2$?

### Solution

Here:

- $p = 0.3$
- desired overall speedup is $S = 2$

Use the general Amdahl’s Law formula:

$$
S = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{k}}
$$

Substitute the values:

$$
2 = \frac{1}{0.7 + \frac{0.3}{k}}
$$

Therefore:

$$
0.7 + \frac{0.3}{k} = 0.5
$$

$$
\frac{0.3}{k} = -0.2
$$

This is impossible, since $\frac{0.3}{k}$ cannot be negative for a positive speedup factor.

Another way to see this is that even if $M$ became infinitely fast, the best possible overall speedup would be:

$$
\frac{1}{0.7} \approx 1.43
$$

which is still less than $2$.

### Final answer

$$
\text{Impossible}
$$

It is impossible to double the overall speed by improving a part that originally accounts for only $30\%$ of the runtime.

## 6. Question (c)

### Question
> Suppose method $M$ can be sped up three-fold. What fraction of the total execution time must $M$ account for in order to double the overall speed of the program?

### Solution

Here:

- speedup of $M$ is $k = 3$
- desired overall speedup is $S = 2$

Use Amdahl’s Law:

$$
2 = \frac{1}{(1-p) + \frac{p}{3}}
$$

Multiply both sides by the denominator:

$$
2\left((1-p) + \frac{p}{3}\right) = 1
$$

$$
2 - 2p + \frac{2p}{3} = 1
$$

$$
1 = 2p - \frac{2p}{3}
$$

$$
1 = p\left(2 - \frac{2}{3}\right)
$$

$$
1 = p \cdot \frac{4}{3}
$$

$$
p = \frac{3}{4}
$$

### Final answer

$$
p = \frac{3}{4} = 75\%
$$

So method $M$ must account for $75\%$ of the original execution time in order for a three-fold improvement in $M$ to double the speed of the whole program.
