Compare wall-clock time for the same program on the two machines. Let $p$ be the parallel portion of uniprocessor run time under Amdahl’s model: the fraction of time the application spends in work that can run in parallel on many cores (so $1-p$ is the inherently serial portion). Normalize so uniprocessor time is $1$; then that serial slice has duration $(1-p)$ and the parallel slice has duration $p$ on that baseline.

Uniprocessor (5 zillion instructions per second): $T_{\mathrm{uni}} = 1$.

Multiprocessor (10 cores at 1 zillion instructions per second each):

- Serial work runs on one core at 1 zillion/s, so it is five times slower than on the 5 zillion/s uniprocessor for the same instructions. The serial slice that took time $(1-p)$ on the uni therefore takes $5(1-p)$ on the MP.
- Parallel work uses all ten cores: 10 zillion/s aggregate vs 5 zillion/s on the uni, so that slice takes time $p/2$ on the MP instead of $p$.

So (same normalization):

$$
T_{\mathrm{mp}} = 5(1-p) + \frac{p}{2}.
$$

Speedup of the multiprocessor relative to the uniprocessor is $S = T_{\mathrm{uni}} / T_{\mathrm{mp}}$:

$$
S = \frac{1}{5(1-p) + \dfrac{p}{2}} = \frac{2}{10(1-p) + p}.
$$

**Decision rule (time only).** The multiprocessor is faster than the uniprocessor exactly when $S > 1$, i.e. when $T_{\mathrm{mp}} < T_{\mathrm{uni}} = 1$:

$$
5(1-p) + \frac{p}{2} < 1.
$$

Multiply by $2$ to clear the fraction:

$$
10(1-p) + p < 2.
$$

Expand and collect terms in $p$:

$$
10 - 10p + p < 2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 8 < 9p \quad\Longrightarrow\quad p > \frac{8}{9}.
$$

So buy the multiprocessor when the parallel time share exceeds $\dfrac{8}{9}$; prefer the uniprocessor when $p < \dfrac{8}{9}$; they tie at $p = \dfrac{8}{9}$. Slow single-thread performance on the MP hurts unless almost all time is parallel.

In this toy comparison, needing $p > \dfrac{8}{9}$ is a strong requirement: many programs spend more than about $\dfrac{1}{9}$ of uni time in serial phases (startup, coordination, locks, single-thread libraries), so the time-only rule often favors the **faster single-thread** machine. Even slightly above the crossover, $S$ is only modest until $p$ is very close to $1$ (fully parallel, $S = 2$ at $p = 1$). Real workloads also rarely match ideal $p$ or linear scaling, so the gap between models and measured speedup is usually nontrivial.

If other factors matter (cost, power, memory), you would compare those at a workload whose $p$ you estimate from profiling or scaling studies.
